## THE FINDING THAT DECIDES IT

The owner's model is: check the clip at submission, tell the clipper which check failed, they fix it, nobody opens the video.

I measured all 635 real rejections. The model inverts the actual data.

| What the owner rejects for | Count | Share | Can a machine see it? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Something **inside the video frame** (logo placement, card layout, story arc, chart visibility, branding covered) | ~174 | 27% | **No.** Needs eyes. |
| **No reason given at all** | 117 | 18.4% | n/a (see final note) |
| **Video is gone** | 113 | 17.8% | **Yes, trivially.** |
| Fraud words ("botted", "Botting", "Bot views") | 76 | 12.0% | Forbidden ground (BL-518) |
| **Caption / hashtag actionable** | **≤35** | **≤5.5%** | Yes, if a rule existed |
| Sound / audio | 23 | 3.6% | Partly |

The single check the owner imagined ("your caption needs #brandname") is **at most 5.5% of the rejection surface**, and that 5.5% is a generous upper bound: my regex counts any reason mentioning a `#tag`. The dominant rejection, 20 times on one campaign alone, is: *"The SomeSome logo/overlay must be placed in the clean middle split area only, like it is part of the app UI."* That is a judgement about pixels. No proposed check sees a pixel.

**And the ground truth is wrong.** The `somesome` campaign's stored rules say:

* `hashtagRules` = `#somesome`
* `captionRules` = `Caption must include: #somesome (This is for Youtube Instagram and Tiktok)`

The owner's live rejections on that same campaign say:

* *"edit caption and insteaf of #somesome type #somesomeapp"* (x2)
* *"edit caption and add #somesomeapp and then i will accept the video"* (x3)
* *"Caption must include: #somesomeapp"* (x1)
* *"Add the somesomeapp hastag"* (x1)

An auto-reviewer reading `campaign.hashtagRules` today would have told roughly seven clippers **"your caption is correct"** and then watched the owner reject them for exactly that. The failure mode is not "the machine is a bit wrong". It is **the machine confidently certifies the precise thing the human rejects**, because it faithfully implemented a stale field.

**There is also almost nothing to read.** Of 12 active campaigns: **3** carry any `hashtagRules` text (`WinGram` "must put #WinGramApp", `somesome` "#somesome" (stale), `GainzAlgo (REPOST)` "#gainzalgo"). The other 9 are null or empty string. `videoLengthMin` / `videoLengthMax` / `bannedContent`: **zero campaigns populate them.** And the real rules live outside the fields entirely: *"read requriements https://docs.google.com/document/d/..."* (x3), *"Add the required comment: 'Download the somesome app on apple/play store'"* (x2), and one that ends *"...and then dm on discord bane234"*.

Nothing machine-reads these fields today. `captionRules` / `hashtagRules` / `videoLengthM*` appear in 12 files, all campaign CRUD and display. **Zero hits in any clip submit or review path.**

**And no caption is ever fetched or stored.** The `Clip` model has no field for caption, description, hashtags, duration, sound or author handle (`prisma/schema.prisma:798-975`). The scraper returns `views/likes/comments/shares/createdAt/platform` plus `raw`, and `raw` is never persisted (`src/lib/apify.ts:242-250`). No provider reads a caption. **The one input every imagined check needs does not exist anywhere in this system.**

## HOW AN AUTO-REVIEWER COEXISTS WITH BL-518 AND BL-521

**The rule, precisely.** BL-518: fraud detection is non-acting; a score alone can no longer change any clip's status, earnings, tracking, or anything a clipper sees. It surgically removed the three automatic status flips (`tracking.ts:1840`, `tracking.ts:996`, `bot-alert.ts:118`). BL-521: a clipper never sees `FLAGGED` or any fraud field; the choke point is `src/lib/clip-sanitize.ts` (`clipperStatus()` maps FLAGGED to PENDING, `sanitizeClipForClipper()` strips 10 fields).

**They coexist on exactly one condition, and it is a clean one:**

> Fraud detection answers a question **about a person** ("is this clipper honest?"). A reviewer may only ever answer questions **about the world** ("is this video reachable? does this caption contain this literal string?"). The reviewer must be structurally incapable of forming the first sentence.

That is not a policy, it is a design constraint, and it yields four hard rules:

1. **The reviewer never writes `Clip.status`.** Not even benignly. BL-518 deleted an automatic status writer; re-adding one rebuilds the exact machinery it deleted, and the next reader cannot tell a benign writer from a fraud one. Auto-reject is therefore **not a product decision, it is a reversal of BL-518**, and must be argued as one.
2. **The reviewer never writes `rejectionReason`.** Measured: that field already carries the owner's fraud words 76 times ("botted", "Botting"). It is the one clipper-visible free-text channel that already carries fraud language, it is rendered verbatim to the clipper (`ClipCardNew.tsx:344-349`) and into the rejection email (`email.ts:592`), and it has **no server-side validation, no trim, no length cap** (`review/route.ts:1161`, `:1201`). Give a machine write access to that field and the two channels merge.
3. **The reviewer's vocabulary is a closed enum of pre-written strings.** Never free text, never a score, never a number, never a confidence. It has no read access to `fraudScore` and nothing derived from it.
4. **The reviewer's output lives in its own namespace**, and joins `CLIPPER_HIDDEN_CLIP_FIELDS`' discipline: a new clipper-facing field is a new leak surface until proven otherwise.

**The second leak channel risk is concrete, not theoretical.** Three measured facts:

* `notifications.ts:518` is the single **write** choke point (87 call sites, all route through it), but it is **not a policy choke point**: no role gate, no type-to-recipient matrix, no runtime type validation (`Notification.type` is a plain `String` column; the union is compile-time only).
* **`metadata` is returned RAW to the clipper.** `getNotifications` does `findMany` with no `select`; `GET /api/notifications` (`route.ts:66-70`) ships every column. This has leaked **twice already** and the file documents both (`notifications.ts:770-774`, `:794-799`: an owner's free-text reason, and a ratio a clipper could recover by subtraction).
* A reviewer notification carrying `{checkId, score, confidence}` in `metadata` is **exactly the shape of both prior incidents.**

**One correction to the record, because it matters for any new guard.** BL-521 states CLIP_FLAGGED is owner-only. Substantively the guarantee holds, but **not for the stated reason, and the type is not owner-only**. `CLIP_FLAGGED` is explicitly overloaded as a community/ticket bucket (`schema.prisma:1755`), and clippers receive it from three sites (`tickets/[id]/messages/route.ts:192`, `channels/[id]/messages/route.ts:424`, `calls/route.ts:148`). What actually enforces the guarantee is that **the FLAGGED branch calls nothing at all**: no `createNotification`, no `publishToUser` (`review/route.ts:1404-1453`, with the deliberate comment at `:1448-1452`). That is a guarantee **by omission inside one `else if`**, not an enforced invariant. Any new guard keyed on the string `CLIP_FLAGGED` would both miss real fraud paths and wrongly catch ticket replies. Worth knowing before anyone adds one.

## PART 1: WHAT THE CLIPPER SEES

### ALL PASS: it goes to the owner's queue. It must not auto-approve.

Approval is where money is stamped: `feePercentAtApproval`, the CPM freeze, the economic snapshot (`pricingModel/minViews/maxPayoutPerClip/ownerUserId ...AtApproval`), and BL-541's owner-share maths. **Auto-approve turns the machine's PASS into an irreversible money write with no human in it.**

But the decisive argument is the measurement, not the money: **a PASS is not evidence of a good clip.** It is evidence that the ≤5.5% we can check is fine. Auto-approving on PASS would auto-approve the 174 logo-placement and card-layout clips the owner rejects. The machine's PASS and the owner's approval are not the same question, and the data says they barely overlap.

**So what does the clipper see on PASS? Nothing new.** The clip stays PENDING, which the UI already renders as "Pending" on the card (`ClipCardNew.tsx:55-59`) and "In review" on the filter chip (`ClipsPremium.tsx:20-25`). That is already exactly the honest statement. Adding "Checks passed" invites the clipper to read it as "approved", and we would be promising on 5.5% of the evidence. **The design that costs nothing and lies about nothing is the one already shipped.**

### SOMETHING FAILS: plain words, on the card, and the clip stays PENDING.

Copy shape: **"Your caption needs #somesomeapp. Add it, then press Re-check."** Never a check number, never a score, never "suspicious", never a count of prior failures.

The failure is **advisory**. The clip does not move. The machine says what it found; the owner still decides. This is the only shape compatible with BL-518, and it happens to be free: the surface already exists and already has the right tone. `ClipCardNew.tsx:241-245` renders *"This video appears to be unavailable. Earnings are paused until the video is accessible again."* **That is already an automated check, told to the clipper in plain language, that changes no status and accuses nobody.** The pattern is shipped. Extend that block (`whitespace-pre-wrap break-words rounded-lg ... px-3 py-2 text-xs` + `autoLinkify`, matching the reason block at `:344-355`), match `ClipCardNew`'s inline status pill rather than `ui/badge.tsx` (which only the dead legacy branch uses), and build no new surface.

### UNKNOWN: the clipper sees nothing, and it costs them nothing.

**The house already does this, in production, and got it right.** The submit freshness gate fails **open** three ways and **refunds the rate limit** every time: all retries fail (`route.ts:993-996`), IG/YT return no `createdAt` (`:1013-1023`), and TikTok returns no `createdAt` (`:1024-1026`, logged *"TikTok createdAt unavailable, allowing (lenient)"*). The reviewer inherits that pattern exactly: **UNKNOWN is silent, is never shown, refunds the press, and routes to the human queue.** Our blind spot never becomes the clipper's problem. This is settled precedent, not a new invention.

### The re-check button, honestly

**What a press actually costs.** The submit path already makes this exact synchronous call: `fetchClipFreshnessWithRetry` (`route.ts:959`), 3 attempts, `[500, 2000, 5000]` backoff (`apify.ts:2117`), holding the request open 3 to 15 seconds. Measured cost (BL-540): `apidojo/tiktok-scraper` **$0.0003/item**, YouTube **$0.00** (free quota), Instagram is **74%** of the real $168/month bill. So a press is fractions of a cent to a few cents. **The real cost is Instagram exposure and a held connection, not the invoice.**

**What stops 500 presses: the mechanism already exists, and it is better than a cooldown.** Submit is rate limited **12/hr** (120/hr with `User.clipSubmitBypass`) at `route.ts:743-762`, **and it refunds the limit when the fetch fails through no fault of the clipper**. Re-check should reuse that exact budget, not invent a parallel one. Recommended:

* Re-check **shares the clipper's existing hourly budget**. It stops at 12. An honest clipper never reaches it, because failures refund.
* **Refund on UNKNOWN or provider failure.** Always. Non negotiable, and already the house pattern.
* A short **cooldown (~60s)** per clip, because a caption edit takes longer than that to propagate anyway (see stale metadata below).
* A **soft per-clip cap (~5)**. Past that the copy is not working, which is **a signal for the owner, not a fee for the clipper**.

**Never charge money per re-check.** It converts our blind spot into their bill and is precisely the "punished for our blind spot" failure. The honest answer to "what stops someone pressing it 500 times" is: the hourly budget stops them at 12, it costs a few cents, and it is visible.

## PART 2: THE FAILURE MODES THAT MATTER

### The machine is wrong and rejects a good clip

**If the reviewer only advises, this failure mode does not exist, and the appeal path is already built: it is the owner's queue.** That is the strongest structural argument for advise-only, and it is worth pricing.

If the machine *could* reject, an appeal path is **net-new surface**, because none exists: there is **no per-clip detail page** (no `src/app/(app)/clips/[id]/`), **no resubmit, edit, retry or delete affordance on any card** (`ClipCardNew` renders exactly one interactive element, the thumbnail link at `:250-255`), and no clipper-to-owner clip-level channel. Advise-only costs zero surface. Auto-reject costs an appeal system.

**The mandatory, obvious path (required either way):** the FAIL block carries one always-available press, **"Think this is wrong? Send it to review anyway."** It routes the clip to the human queue with the machine's verdict attached and marked disputed. Never rate limited, never charged, never hidden behind the re-check budget. And it doubles as the misfire tripwire (Part 3).

### Stale metadata: they fixed it, the platform has not caught up

**This is the common case, not the edge case.** The clipper edits the caption; our next fetch may still return the old one. Instagram is a known refuser, not a known failer (BL-545: *"HikerAPI is not failing, it is refusing"*), and the submit path already bypasses HikerAPI because it returns `createdAt: null` (`apify.ts:2162-2167`).

**Rule: a re-check that returns byte-identical metadata to the previous check is UNKNOWN, not FAIL.** Never re-fail on identical bytes. Say *"We're still seeing the old caption. Give it a few minutes and press Re-check,"* and refund the press. A clipper is never told they failed because of our propagation lag.

**And a real bug falls out of this, worth fixing regardless of whether the reviewer is ever built.** The 30 minute posting window (`clip-config.ts:13`, `MAX_CLIP_AGE_MS`) means **"fix it and resubmit" is advice the platform cannot honour.** Dedup explicitly permits it (`route.ts:1054-1081` scopes to PENDING/APPROVED, comment: *"re-submit after rejection allowed"*) but the freshness gate hard-blocks it 30 minutes later (`route.ts:1005-1011`). Meanwhile the rejection notification tells every rejected clipper *"Check the reason and try again"* (`review/route.ts:1266`), and **35 rejection reasons literally instruct the clipper to edit the caption and resubmit** ("edit caption and add #somesomeapp and then i will accept the video"). **That instruction is refused by the submit path.** Those clippers cannot comply. Re-check on the existing clip row is the fix for a broken promise that already exists, today, with or without a machine.

### The rule is ambiguous free text

**It is worse than ambiguous. It is empty or wrong** (measured above: 3 of 12 campaigns carry any hashtag text; one of those three certifies the exact string the owner rejects; `videoLengthMin`/`bannedContent` are populated on zero campaigns; real rules sit in a Google Doc and a Discord DM).

So the honest answer to *"what happens when the interpretation is arguable"* is: **it is not arguable yet, because there is nothing to interpret.** Any machine reading those fields today is confidently wrong. The prerequisite for a caption check is **not a better interpreter. It is a rule the owner maintains and the machine can check literally.**

Recommendation: a structured `requiredHashtags String[]` per campaign, edited by the owner, checked by **literal string containment**. Never prose interpretation. The moment the rule is structured and literal, the ambiguity problem **disappears**, because the machine is no longer interpreting. That is the whole point. **And if the owner will not maintain that field, the check must not exist**. An unmaintained rule field is how you get the `#somesome` failure at scale.

### A clipper games the checks: adds the tag to pass, removes it after approval

Detectable, cheaply: tracking already re-polls every clip on its `:00` cadence, so a caption re-read rides an existing call at roughly $0 on TikTok/YouTube.

**But do not build it, and be clear why.** Three reasons, in ascending order of importance:

1. **Nobody is doing it.** 0 of 635 rejections mention a removed or reverted hashtag. Building an anti-gaming detector for a behaviour with zero observed instances, *before* building the check it protects, is inverted.
2. **The owner loses little.** Earnings are cumulative-recomputed, so a post-approval removal is not a money event unless the owner acts, and the only lever is a manual status change anyway, because BL-518 forbids the machine acting.
3. **It is a suspicion detector.** "Did they remove the tag after approval?" answers a question **about a person**. It is exactly the shape BL-518 forbids from acting and BL-521 forbids from being seen. If it is ever built, it is owner-only, silent and non-acting: **identical to `fraud.ts`. Which means it belongs in the fraud queue, not the reviewer, and must be named that way**, so that nobody later mistakes it for reviewer advice and shows it to a clipper.

## PART 3: WHAT THE OWNER SEES

### What replaces opening the video: measured, nothing does. Yet.

27% of rejections are judgements about pixels (logo placement x21+, card layout x13, story arc x3, branding covered x3, chart half-visible x2, *"White line in the logo"* x3). **The owner will still open those videos.** The honest framing is not "the reviewer replaces watching". It is:

> **The reviewer removes the clips that never needed opening.**

Measured, that is the **113 "video is gone" rejections**, where a human opened a video only to discover there was no video.

### The queue: the reviewer's real product is ORDERING, not deciding.

BL-519 already established the pattern on this exact surface: a **read-only shadow ranking** layered on the live queue so the owner reads a better-sorted list without touching the live path. The reviewer should be that, and nothing more. Three groups:

* **Nothing to open** (video gone, or a literal check failed and the clipper has already been told). Owner presses once.
* **Needs your eyes** (everything else, which is most of it, and the dashboard should say so).
* **Machine unsure** (UNKNOWN). Handled identically to "needs your eyes", but **labelled**, so the owner can watch the blind-spot rate directly.

Per clip: which checks ran, what each returned, and **when it last ran**. Never a score. Never a verdict about the person.

### Spotting a misfire before 200 clippers are wrongly rejected

**BL-524's rule bites hardest here, and BL-526 is the worked example on this very system.** 15 false `CRON_DOWN` emails fired because a `null` last-fired timestamp (a check that **never ran**) was indistinguishable from a stale one (a check that **died**). Quiet read as broken.

**The inverse is the danger here, and it is live today.** A check whose rule field is empty **silently passes everything**, and that is true of **9 of 12 campaigns right now**. A check passing everything because it has no rule looks *identical* to a check working and finding nothing. Both are green. So:

* Per check, per campaign, show: **ran / passed / failed / unknown**, and **"not configured" as a first-class state**. A check with no rule renders **"No rule set. This check is not running."** It must **never** render a green tick.
* **The misfire tripwire has a known baseline:** ≤5.5% of rejections are caption-actionable. If a check's fail rate on a campaign exceeds a small multiple of that within an hour, it is misfiring. Alert, and **auto-suspend that one check** (not the reviewer).
  * Stated honestly: auto-suspending a check **is** a machine acting. But it acts **on the machine, not on a clip**. It removes advice. It cannot touch a status, a clipper, or money, and it fails toward the human. That is the only automatic action I would allow anywhere in this design.
* **The disputed-press count is the cheapest misfire signal that exists.** 200 wrongly rejected clippers generate 200 "Think this is wrong?" presses long before the owner notices a pattern by eye. Put that on the dashboard **as a rate**, and alert on it.

### The kill switch

One click, per check and global. A **DB flag read on every evaluation**: not an env var (needs a deploy), not a code constant. Off means the reviewer emits **nothing to anyone**: no card text, no queue grouping, no notification.

**And here is the part worth stating plainly:** because the reviewer only ever advises, **the kill switch has no money consequence and no status consequence.** Killing it cannot strand a clip, cannot unpay anyone, cannot re-freeze an era. It is safe *precisely because the thing it kills never had power.*

**"How would the owner know at 2am?"** They would not, and they should not need to, **because at 2am the machine has rejected nobody.** The worst a misfiring advise-only reviewer does overnight is show wrong advice and mis-sort a queue. That is the entire point of advise-only: **it converts a 2am emergency into a morning cleanup.** If the machine could auto-reject, this section would need a pager. It does not.

## PART 4: THE HONEST VERDICT

### No check should auto-reject. Not one. Not even the easy one.

**On R-2's false rejection rate: it does not exist.** R-2 has not landed. I will not argue from a number I do not have. What I *can* argue from is measured here:

* **The machine's ground truth is provably wrong today.** `somesome`'s stored rule certifies the exact string the owner rejects. **This has a direct consequence for R-2:** a false rejection rate measured against `campaign.hashtagRules` would measure *agreement with a stale field*, not correctness. It would report a beautiful number and mean nothing. **Any FRR must be measured against the owner's actual decisions, or it is worthless.** R-2 should be told this before it runs.
* **The reviewer can see at most 5.5% of the rejection surface.** Even a *perfect* check leaves 94.5% needing a human.
* **Advice with a 94.5% blind spot is useful. Authority with a 94.5% blind spot is not.**

### What would have to be true before a machine may reject an honest clipper's work

1. **The rule is structured data the owner maintains**, not prose. Today: 3 of 12 campaigns carry any hashtag text, and one of the three is wrong.
2. **The check's disagreement with the owner is measured, not modelled**, over a real period on real clips, in **shadow mode**, and is near zero. The reviewer runs silently, logs what it *would* have said, and **the owner's actual decision is the label**. This is BL-519's shadow pattern again, and it is the only honest way to produce R-2's FRR.
3. **The clipper can reverse it in one press**, and that press reaches a human quickly.
4. **The failure is reversible with no money consequence.** And this one is fatal on inspection: approval **is** the money stamp, so a machine may only act *before* approval, and "acting" before approval can only mean "not yet approved", which is **PENDING**, which is **where the clip already is**. **There is no rejection a machine can perform that is both useful and reversible. Auto-reject has no safe version.**
5. **BL-518 would have to be relaxed**, explicitly and by the owner. Auto-reject *is* an automatic system changing a clip's status. That the reason is "missing hashtag" rather than "fraud" does not matter: **the rule BL-518 enforced is structural, not thematic.** The two blocks it removed from `tracking.ts` looked benign to whoever wrote them too.

### The smallest first version that is genuinely useful

**"Your video is gone."** One check. Zero interpretation. Zero fraud adjacency. Zero new rule authoring. Nothing to maintain.

* **Measured value: 113 rejections (17.8%)**, the largest *named* reason after "no reason at all", where a human opened a video to find nothing there. And the sharpest fact in this report: **`videoUnavailable` is `false` on all 113.** The detector exists and caught **none of them**. A human did the machine's job 113 times.
* **It answers a question about the world, not about the clipper.** It cannot leak a judgement because there is no judgement to leak. It is the one check that is structurally incapable of violating BL-518 or BL-521.
* **The UI already exists and already has the right tone:** `ClipCardNew.tsx:241-245`. Extend that block. Build no new surface.
* **Re-check is genuinely useful here**, because unavailability is often transient (a private-to-public toggle, a processing delay). The cost is one fetch the tracking cron would make anyway.
* **It never rejects.** It groups the clip into "Nothing to open" with a one-press reject. The owner still presses.
* **Shadow it first anyway.** Log what it would have said for a week; compare against the owner's real rejections; only then show a clipper.

**Second version, only if the owner will maintain the data:** a structured `requiredHashtags` array plus literal containment. Shadow-measured against the owner's decisions, never against the rule field. **Not before.**

**Do not build first:** the caption interpreter (no caption is fetched, and nowhere to put one), the length check (0 campaigns set `videoLengthMin`), the banned-content check (0 campaigns set `bannedContent`), and anything that asks whether the clipper is honest.

### One last thing, and it may be the biggest trust win available

**The largest single bucket of rejections is 117 clips (18.4%) with no reason at all.** Those clippers saw a red "Rejected" pill and nothing else. The card renders the reason block only when `rejectionReason` is non-empty (`ClipCardNew.tsx:344`), so they got silence.

And **76 rejections tell the clipper "botted" / "Botting" / "Bot views" in free text.** That is the platform's own fraud judgement, delivered to a clipper by hand, through the one clipper-visible field BL-521 never audited **because a human types it**. It is the owner's call what to say to their own clippers, and I am not calling it a rule violation: BL-518 and BL-521 bind the *machine*. But it is worth the owner seeing plainly that a round was spent proving a clipper can never see a fraud hint, while 76 rejection reasons say "botted" in plain text, rendered verbatim to the card and into the email (`email.ts:592`).

**Before building a machine to explain machine decisions, the platform could explain its human ones.** 117 silent rejections and 76 blunt ones is a bigger trust problem than the absence of an auto-reviewer, it is measured, and it needs no machine to fix.
