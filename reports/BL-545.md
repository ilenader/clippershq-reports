## THE HEADLINE: HIKERAPI IS NOT FAILING. IT IS REFUSING CORRECTLY, AND IT IS ALREADY FREE.

**BL-544 was wrong, and it was my own round, so let me correct it plainly.** BL-544 said *"2,128 failures, and EVERY failure falls through to a billed Apify actor."* **That is false.**

Measured post-fix (2026-07-03 onward, 15,947 HikerAPI calls):

| | Count | Share of failures |
|---|---|---|
| Success | 12,059 (**75.6%**) | |
| **`gone_404` → verdict "gone" → SKIPS Apify → costs $0** | **3,219** | **82.8%** |
| `null_views_on_200` → falls through to Apify | 346 | 8.9% |
| `classification_not_reel_single` (genuine 200-OK carousels) | 194 | 5.0% |
| unlabelled | 91 | 2.3% |
| `5xx` | 35 | 0.9% |
| `network` | 3 | 0.1% |
| **Total that actually reaches a billed Apify actor** | **669** | **17.2%** |

**83% of HikerAPI's "failures" are correct refusals on deleted or inaccessible posts, and they already skip Apify for free.** The system does exactly what the brief hoped it would: it recognises a permanent "this post no longer exists" answer and stops asking.

**The real HikerAPI defect rate is 669 / 15,947 = 4.2%, not 25.7%.**

**The "25.7% failure rate" is a labelling artifact.** `recordHikerUsage("fallback", …)` writes `success=false` for `gone_404` even though it is a correct, intended, zero-cost outcome. The ledger counts a right answer as a failure, and BL-544 (me) read that number without opening it.

**And the fix the brief is asking for already shipped.** BL-275 fixed exactly this, and the data shows the day it landed.

| Bucket | Post-fix count | Reaches Apify? | Verdict |

**The breaker has never fired, so it has never diverted a single call to Apify.** The hypothesis that a burst of legitimate refusals trips the breaker and sends healthy clips to paid Apify is **false**: `gone_404` deliberately does **not** call `checkAndMaybeFireOutage()` (`hikerapi.ts:697` states this outright — *"a 404 is NOT a Hiker outage (the video is gone, the provider is fine)"*), and no 429 or 402 has ever occurred.

HikerAPI has never been rate-limited and has never run out of balance. The breaker is dormant, correct, and cost-free.

## PART 3 — REPEAT OFFENDERS: SMALL, AND ALREADY CHEAP

**I could not identify which clips failed.** `hikerDiag` records only `{kind, outcome, httpStatus}` — **no clipId, no URL, and `campaignId` is null on every Hiker row.** So "how many distinct clips account for the failures" is **not answerable from the ledger**. I will not guess it. Approaching from the clip side instead:

| Measure | Count |
|---|---|
| Tracking jobs total / active | 3,927 / 3,869 |
| **Active jobs on `videoUnavailable` clips** | **6** |
| Active jobs with ≥3 consecutive failures | 68 |
| Active jobs with ≥10 consecutive failures | **0** (worst is **4**) |
| **IG jobs parked on `DEAD_72H` at 4320 min, still active** | **244** |
| IG jobs on `AUTO_PAUSED` at 4320 min | 441 |

**Does the system ever stop asking? Effectively yes, twice over.** The auto-ladder parks dead links at `DEAD_72H` / 4320 min (72h), and `gone_404` then skips Apify on every one of those polls. **244 dead IG clips polling every 72h is ~81 polls/day, and each costs HikerAPI's $0.001 and nothing else — about $2.44/month, and $0.00 of Apify.**

**So "the same dead clips re-checked forever" costs roughly $2.44/month, not a meaningful share of the bill.** Pre-BL-275 it would have been ~250 billed Apify actor calls per day. That is the difference the fix made.

The honest gap: only **6** active jobs sit on `videoUnavailable` clips, yet `gone_404` fires ~250 times/day. So most 404s are on clips **not** flagged `videoUnavailable` — the ladder parks them at 72h but never marks them dead. **Whether a 404 should eventually set `videoUnavailable` and deactivate the job is a real open question**, worth ~$2.44/month and some wasted polls. It is not worth a project.

## PART 4 — SUBMIT AND ACCOUNT CONNECTION (nobody had mapped this)

### A clipper submits a clip

`src/actions/clips.ts:71-80`:

```ts
if (platform === "tiktok" || platform === "instagram") {
  const fetchOut = await fetchClipStats(data.clipUrl);
```

| Platform | What fires | Provider | Billed? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Instagram** | `fetchClipStats` → the full chain | **HikerAPI first**, Apify only on miss | **Only on a Hiker miss** |
| **TikTok** | `fetchClipStats` → `apidojo/tiktok-scraper` tier 1, `clockworks` tier 2 | Apify | **YES** |
| **YouTube** | **nothing** — the branch excludes it | none | **no** |

It takes a **first view count** at submit and the same call serves the 30-minute posting-window freshness check. **Frequency: once per submission.** **Instagram submissions are already on the cheap provider.** TikTok submit is on a billed Apify actor and is the one place a cheaper vendor (TikHub or LamaTok, both verified by BL-544) could take a small saving — but it fires once per clip, so the volume is submissions/day, not polls/day.

### A clipper connects an account and their bio is verified

**This splits by platform and the answer is better than expected.**

| Platform | Tier 1 | Provider | Billed? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **TikTok / YouTube** | `checkBioForCode` → **plain fetch** (`verify/route.ts:666`) | **NONE — a plain HTTP fetch** | **NO. FREE.** |
| **Instagram** | direct Apify call (`verify/route.ts:233`) | `apify/instagram-profile-scraper` via `run-sync-get-dataset-items` | **YES** |

`verify-cascade.ts` Tier 2/3 (apidojo profile → Browserless) fire **only on a Tier-1 miss** and are capped at `TIER23_DAILY_CAP = 10`/day (`verify-cascade.ts:94`). Tier 1 short-circuits on a hit.

**Cost: BL-540 measured `apify/instagram-profile-scraper` at 13 runs / $0.03 per 3.5 days ≈ $0.26/month.** Frequency: **once per account, ever.**

**So the answer to the owner is: bios are already nearly free.** TikTok and YouTube bios cost **nothing** and use **no vendor at all**. Instagram bio is the only billed one, at about **26 cents a month**. TikHub could take it (BL-540 verified the `biography` field), but **migrating it is effort for a rounding error**, exactly as BL-540 said. **This was never the problem, and the owner's worry about it is unfounded.**

Two honest notes: the IG bio call at `verify/route.ts:233` **bypasses the provider chain and the ledger entirely** (`logApifyUsage` is never called), so it is invisible in `apify_usage_entries` — an observability gap worth $0.26/month, not money. And it calls Apify **directly by actor id**, not through `fetchClipStats`, so it would not benefit from any chain-level migration.

### The owner submits on someone's behalf (override path)

`owner-submit-core.ts` calls `fetchClipStats` on the same chain as a normal submit, so the provider answer is identical: **IG on HikerAPI-first, TikTok on billed Apify, YouTube not scraped.** **BL-541 holds this file; I only read it and changed nothing.**

## PART 6 — THE VERDICT AND THE ONE NEXT STEP

**What is HikerAPI actually failing on?** Almost nothing. **83% of its "failures" are deleted or inaccessible Instagram posts that it correctly refuses and that already skip Apify at zero cost.** Its true defect rate is **4.2%**, and the largest genuine bucket (`null_views_on_200`, ~25/day) is Hiker **correctly refusing to return a view count it does not trust** rather than returning a wrong one. **This is correct behaviour, not a defect.**

**How much of the $74/month is avoidable for free? Essentially none of it, because BL-275 already collected it on 2026-07-02.** The free win the brief is reaching for — *"recognise a permanent 'this post no longer exists' answer and STOP asking"* — is **already implemented** (BL-262 for the verdict, BL-275 for the ordering) and the data shows it working: 189 to 339 wasted Apify calls/day went to **zero** overnight. Re-checking dead clips now costs about **$2.44/month**, all of it HikerAPI, none of it Apify.

**So there is no five-line fix worth $74/month here, and I am not going to invent one.**

**The honest finding is that the $74/month itself is unverified.** `apify-instagram-api-only` is a **batch-level label** whose `clipCount` records the **whole batch**, not the URLs actually sent to Apify after Hiker's hits and gone-skips were removed. Its daily clip count tracks HikerAPI's call count almost exactly (1,085/1,017, 1,522/1,504, 1,491/1,485), which is what a whole-batch counter looks like. **I nearly concluded from that correlation that Hiker's successes were not preventing Apify calls at all. The code says they are** (`apify.ts:1322`: `urlsForApify = missingForApify`, and the Apify call is skipped entirely when that list is empty). **The ledger simply cannot tell you which of the two is true**, and BL-540 already flagged the same class of defect from the other direction (the ledger under-reports Apify by 32% because it bills only the winning provider).

### The single highest-value next action

**Fix the ledger's per-provider attribution so the Instagram bill can be attributed at all.** Record, per batch, `urls.length` versus `urlsForApify.length` versus `hikerHits` versus `hikerGoneSkipped` — every one of those numbers is **already computed and already logged to Railway** in the `[SCRAPER-DECISION]` line at `apify.ts:1330`. It is not in the database, so nobody can query it.

This is free, it is read-only observability, it carries no money-path risk, and **until it exists, every statement about how much Instagram costs and why — including BL-540's $74, BL-544's, and mine — rests on a label that cannot distinguish a batch from a billed call.** **BL-543 may hold `apify-ledger.ts`; coordinate before touching it.**

**And nothing here undermines BL-544's core finding: Apify must stay as the fallback.** It is now clearer why. Apify is not catching 2,128 failures a week — it is catching **669**, of which 346 are cases where HikerAPI refused to guess a view count. **Those 669 are exactly the clips whose earnings would freeze if Apify were retired.** The fallback is cheaper than anyone thought and more necessary than the raw failure count suggested.
