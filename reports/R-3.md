### The every-second podcast-matching idea, costed honestly

You asked specifically about scanning every second to match a clip against a source podcast. **Costed as asked, it is absurd. But the idea is right — the method is wrong.**

**As asked** (scan the source per clip): a 2-hour podcast at 1 frame/second = **7,200 frames**. Per clip. × 2,284 clips/month = **16.4 million frames ≈ 26.3 billion tokens**.

> **≈ $26,300/month on Haiku 4.5. About 157× your entire current Apify bill.**

**Done properly** (index once, match cheap): index the podcast **once** — 7,200 frames ≈ 11.5M tokens ≈ **$11.52 one-time per podcast on Haiku**. Then match each clip's frames against that index.

**But do not use an LLM for the matching step at all.** Frame matching is what **perceptual hashing** (pHash/dHash) exists for: compute a 64-bit fingerprint per frame, compare with Hamming distance. It is **CPU-only, sub-millisecond, and $0 per call** — the same technique behind Content ID. An LLM asked "is this the same footage?" is slower, costlier, and *less* reliable than a hash designed for exactly this.

**Verdict: "match against source podcast" is achievable and nearly free — via hashing, not via Claude.** The LLM is the wrong tool for this specific rule.

## PART 3 — WHAT IS ALREADY PAID FOR

### The Claude Max subscription cannot serve product traffic. Stated plainly.

**A Claude Max subscription is a personal plan for interactive use** by a human through claude.ai and Claude Code. **Automated per-clip image analysis inside your product is product traffic, and product traffic requires the Anthropic API with API billing.** Routing your clip pipeline through a personal subscription would be a usage-policy violation, and I am not going to design a plan that depends on it. **This is not a technicality to route around — it is the answer.**

The good news is that it barely matters: **API billing for Rung 1 is $2.23 to $4.45/month.** The subscription question is moot at that price.

### Genuinely free or near-free options — taken seriously

**These deserve more weight than the API, because for your actual rules they are both cheaper and better.**

**1. Perceptual hashing (pHash) — $0/call, the right tool for two of your four rules.**
Logo-present and source-footage-match are *template matching* problems, not comprehension problems. OpenCV template matching finds a known logo's bounding box in milliseconds; pHash matches footage against an indexed source. **No model, no API, no per-call cost — just CPU.** For "is the SomeSome logo on this frame", this is strictly better than an LLM: deterministic, instant, free, and it returns *coordinates*, which is exactly what your placement rule needs.

**2. OCR (Tesseract / PaddleOCR) — $0/call, the right tool for your text rules.**
*"The top right should say 'What should I put on the cards?'"* is OCR plus a string comparison plus a position check. Free, local, deterministic. It also returns **text bounding boxes** — which is the missing half of your logo rule: **logo box ∩ text boxes ≠ ∅ → the logo is over captions → reject.** That single geometric test encodes your highest-count pixel rule (14 rejections), using two free CV tools and no model at all.

**3. A local VLM on your own PC — electricity only.**
16GB comfortably runs a small vision-language model (Qwen2-VL-2B, Moondream, or a quantised LLaVA-7B). **Costs electricity, not per-call fees**, and more RAM would widen the options. This is the right home for the *judgement* rules that hashing and OCR cannot do — *"does the logo look pasted on, or like a real app interface?"*, *"is the orange text a question and the blue text the answer?"* Those need comprehension.

**The honest caveat on local:** a local VLM is slower per clip, needs the PC to be on, and is meaningfully **less accurate** than Claude on subtle judgement. At 76 clips/day (~3/hour) throughput is a non-issue. Accuracy is the real question, and it is unmeasured — **I did not benchmark any local model**, and I will not claim one works on your rules without testing it.

### The honest conclusion, which is not what you were hoping to hear

**Metadata checks capture most of the value for free, and pixel checks are cheap but harder than they look. Both halves of that sentence matter.**

**The line falls in an unexpected place.** It is not "metadata is free, pixels are expensive" — at 2,284 clips/month, pixels are **also nearly free**: $2.23/month batched, or literally $0 with OCR and template matching. **Cost is not your constraint. Correctness is.**

Your pixel rules are not the easy kind. *"Is the logo present"* is trivial. *"Is the logo placed in the clean middle split area and not overlapping any caption, subtitle, or username"* is a spatial-semantic judgement, and **a wrong answer here is worse than no answer**: auto-rejecting a good clip costs a clipper their earnings and your goodwill, and auto-approving a bad one defeats the point. That is why my recommendation is **assistive, not decisive** — surface a flag and a confidence score to your reviewer; do not let it reject anything on its own until you have measured its accuracy against your own labelled history.

**And you have that labelled history already: 325 rejections with reasons, sitting in the database.** That is your test set. Before spending a dollar, run any candidate check against those 325 and see how many it agrees with.
